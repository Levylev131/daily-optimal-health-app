Degenerates ++

You quit vaping. You're drinking water. You're actually trying. This is your next level.

Quitting nicotine is genuinely one of the hardest things a person can do — and you did it. This page builds on that win. The habits here are bigger than before, but still built around your actual lifestyle. You still game. You still sit. That's fine. Let's just make the rest of the day work harder for you.
Time Activity
8:00 AMWake up 30–60 minutes earlier than before. You're not a full morning person yet and that's fine — but quitting nicotine has already improved your sleep quality, which means you're actually getting more out of fewer hours. Use that extra time.Why it matters: without nicotine disrupting REM sleep, your body recovers faster. Waking up slightly earlier now costs you less than it used to.
8:30 AMDrink 20 oz of water immediately — you've already built this habit, keep it locked in. Then step outside for 10 minutes. Not a scroll session in bed. Actual outside. Morning light now, cravings less later — your dopamine system is still recalibrating from nicotine and sunlight helps.Why it matters: nicotine artificially flooded your dopamine system for years. Morning sunlight is one of the fastest natural ways to restore healthy dopamine baseline.
9:00 AMEat a real breakfast with protein. Now that you're not suppressing appetite with nicotine, hunger is real again — feed it properly. Eggs, Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, or a protein shake. Aim for at least 30g of protein to start the day.Why it matters: nicotine suppressed appetite for years. Your metabolism is recalibrating — high protein breakfast reduces junk food cravings throughout the day.
9:30 AMStart work. You'll notice focus feels different without nicotine — some days sharper, some days harder. That's normal and temporary. Replace the "reach for the vape" habit with a sip of water or a piece of gum. The hand-to-mouth reflex takes a few months to fully fade.Why it matters: the hand-to-mouth habit is conditioned behavior separate from nicotine addiction. Having a replacement action prevents relapse more than willpower alone.
10:00 AMFirst workout of your new life — 15 to 20 minutes. No gym required. Bodyweight exercises at your desk: 3 sets of push-ups, 3 sets of squats, and a 1-minute plank. That's it. Your cardiovascular system is already recovering from vaping — now it's time to actually build on that recovery.Why it matters: lung capacity begins recovering within weeks of quitting nicotine. Light exercise accelerates this recovery and the gains come faster than you'd expect.
10:30 AMBack to work — and try 30 minutes of single-focus work. No show, no game in the background. Just the task. You're rebuilding your dopamine system's ability to find reward in normal activities. Deep focus work is one of the best ways to train this.Why it matters: nicotine hijacked your dopamine reward system. Single-focus work rebuilds the brain's ability to find satisfaction in non-stimulant activities — a critical post-nicotine recovery step.
11:00 AMStand up, stretch, and take a 5-minute walk around your space. Your hourly movement habit from before should be locked in by now — this is just maintaining it. Add a set of 15 push-ups or 20 squats before sitting back down.Why it matters: the hourly movement habit is one of the highest-value things you built. Adding reps turns it from a health habit into a fitness habit without needing a separate workout.
11:30 AMDrink water and have a healthy snack — fruit, nuts, or Greek yogurt. Without nicotine suppressing your appetite, you may feel hungrier than before. Plan for snacks so you're not reaching for junk out of desperation.Why it matters: increased appetite after quitting nicotine is real and temporary. Having healthy snacks ready prevents the weight gain that often comes from replacing vaping with junk food.
12:00 PMLunch — step it up from the "one vegetable" rule. Now aim for half your plate being vegetables, protein, or whole foods. You don't have to eat clean all the time — just make lunch the meal where you consistently do well.Why it matters: one high-quality meal per day is a sustainable entry point into better nutrition. It doesn't require overhauling your diet and compounds significantly over months.
12:30 PMPost-lunch walk — 15–20 minutes. You've already built this habit. Now increase the pace slightly. Not a jog — just walk with purpose. Slightly faster walking meaningfully improves cardiovascular fitness over time.Why it matters: increasing walk intensity slightly is the easiest cardiovascular upgrade available. Even a 10% pace increase adds real aerobic benefit without feeling like exercise.
1:00 PMBack to work. Practice the single-focus block again — 25 to 30 minutes of no background noise, no show, no multitasking. Use the Pomodoro method: 25 minutes on, 5 minutes off. Your concentration will rebuild faster than you think once nicotine is fully out of your system.Why it matters: nicotine withdrawal impairs focus for 2–4 weeks but then focus typically exceeds pre-quitting levels. You are building a cognitive engine, not just breaking a habit.
1:30 PMHydrate. Check in with yourself — any nicotine cravings right now? If yes, they peak at 3–5 minutes and then pass. Drink water, do 20 squats, or step outside. Physical movement is one of the fastest craving suppressors available.Why it matters: exercise triggers dopamine release through a healthy pathway, directly competing with nicotine cravings and reducing their intensity over time.
2:00 PMHourly stand and move — this time do something slightly harder. Try 10 burpees, 20 lunges, or a 2-minute wall sit. You're past the beginner stage. Your body can handle more and needs more stimulus to keep improving.Why it matters: your baseline fitness has risen enough that the same 10 squats you started with are no longer a sufficient stimulus. Progressive challenge is required for continued improvement.
2:30 PMAfternoon energy slump — handle it without nicotine and without excess caffeine. Try cold water on your face, a short walk outside, or 5 minutes of deep breathing. These all work. It won't feel as instant as vaping did, but the energy lasts longer and the crash is gone.Why it matters: the afternoon slump is largely caused by the same crash cycle that nicotine created. Without it, the slump is milder and fully manageable with natural tools.
3:00 PMProtein-focused snack. Your muscles are now getting actual stimulation from the movement you've been adding — feed them. Beef jerky, hard-boiled eggs, cottage cheese, or a protein shake. Aim for 20–30g of protein in this window.Why it matters: you are now building muscle with your hourly reps. Without adequate protein, the stimulus goes to waste. Protein timing around movement accelerates results.
3:30 PMIncrease your daily walk target — aim for 7,000 to 8,000 steps today. Track it on your phone. You're probably hitting 4,000–5,000 already from your existing habits. The gap is filled by a longer afternoon walk or simply being more deliberate about movement.Why it matters: 7,000–8,000 steps per day is associated with a 50–65% reduction in all-cause mortality compared to sedentary levels. You're within reach of this threshold.
4:00 PMEnd-of-workday movement — do a proper 10-minute bodyweight circuit before you close the laptop. Push-ups, squats, lunges, and a plank. No equipment, no gym, no commute. Just 10 minutes as a transition between work and gaming.Why it matters: a short circuit at day's end resets cortisol, improves evening mood, and creates a clear mental boundary between work and leisure — which improves both.
4:30 PMGet outside for a proper 20-minute walk before gaming starts. Fresh air, daylight, away from screens. Think of this as your decompression ritual — the thing that makes gaming feel better and more focused because you're not carrying the tension of a sedentary day into it.Why it matters: outdoor light exposure in the afternoon delays melatonin onset slightly, which means you'll feel more awake during evening gaming and sleepier when you actually want to sleep.
5:00 PMEat dinner sitting down, away from a screen. This is now a real rule — not optional. You've proven you can build habits. This one matters for digestion, portion control, and making sure food actually registers as satisfying to your brain.Why it matters: eating without a screen increases meal satisfaction and reduces total calorie intake by 15–25% on average, without trying to eat less.
5:30 PMPost-dinner walk — 15 minutes minimum. You know the drill. This is non-negotiable at your level. Blood sugar, digestion, sleep quality — this one walk improves all three every single night.Why it matters: consistency over months makes this walk one of the most powerful cardiovascular and metabolic habits in your entire routine.
6:00 PMGaming time — earned and enjoyed. Set up your station with water on the desk, your snacks chosen in advance, and your monitor on night mode. You've already done the work today. Game without guilt.Why it matters: guilt-free leisure time is a real health factor. Chronic guilt and stress around habits you enjoy increases cortisol and undermines the health gains you've made elsewhere.
7:00 PMOne glass of water per hour of gaming — you've heard this before and hopefully it's automatic now. Hydration during gaming isn't just health advice, it's a performance tip. Reaction time, decision-making, and focus all degrade with dehydration.Why it matters: at your improved fitness level, you will notice the difference in gaming sharpness between hydrated and dehydrated sessions.
7:30 PMStand up between matches and do something physical — 15 push-ups, a short walk to the kitchen and back, or some shoulder stretches. Wrist and eye health matter more now that gaming is your primary leisure. Protect the hobby.Why it matters: repetitive strain injuries accumulate silently over months. Two-minute breaks between sessions cost nothing and prevent injuries that can sideline you for weeks.
8:00 PMLast food of the night. Make it something with protein — cottage cheese, a protein shake, Greek yogurt, or nuts. Eating stops here. A 12-hour fast overnight (8 PM to 8 AM) supports gut repair, metabolic health, and better sleep quality.Why it matters: time-restricted eating improves insulin sensitivity, supports fat metabolism, and gives your digestive system the overnight rest it needs to function well the next day.
8:30 PMIf you feel a nicotine craving right now, it's likely tied to a gaming trigger — this was a paired habit for a long time. Acknowledge it, drink water, and keep playing. The craving will pass in under 5 minutes. Each time it passes without giving in, the association weakens.Why it matters: conditioned cravings triggered by gaming can persist for months after quitting. Each successful pass-through permanently weakens the neural association.
9:00 PMDim your monitor and switch to night mode if not already on. Your sleep is now genuinely better than it was when you were vaping — protect that win. Blue light is the main remaining threat to your sleep quality at this stage.Why it matters: you've already fixed the biggest sleep disruptor (nicotine). Blue light is now the primary remaining factor limiting your sleep depth and recovery quality.
9:30 PMStart thinking about winding down. You don't have to stop gaming yet — but be conscious of the time. Set a hard stop for 11 PM. Getting to sleep by 11:30 gives you 8+ hours before an 8 AM wake-up, and your body will use every minute of it.Why it matters: your body is now in a recovery phase from years of nicotine damage. Extra sleep during this period accelerates healing of lung tissue, cardiovascular function, and nervous system health.
10:00 PMFinish your current game. No new long sessions after this point. Transition to something lower stimulation — a show, casual browsing, or just lying down. You've upgraded from midnight to 11 PM. That extra hour of sleep is worth more than almost any supplement.Why it matters: each additional hour of sleep before midnight is disproportionately rich in deep sleep and growth hormone release — the body prioritizes its most restorative work early in the night.
10:30 PMWind-down routine. Stretch for 5–10 minutes, dim the lights, put the phone face-down. Reflect on the day — not formally, just briefly. You've built real habits. Recognizing progress, even briefly, reinforces the behavior loop and makes it more durable.Why it matters: self-acknowledgment of progress activates the same reward pathways that nicotine used to hijack. You're reclaiming your own dopamine system.
11:00 PMSleep. Consistent, nicotine-free, reasonably-timed sleep is the foundation everything else is built on. You've done the hard part. The version of you that started on the Degenerates page wouldn't recognize where you are now.Why it matters: at this point your lung function, cardiovascular health, sleep quality, focus, and fitness are all measurably better than they were. This is not a small thing. Keep going.
